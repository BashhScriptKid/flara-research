This is a genuinely difficult spot to be in — you’re trying to be fair to someone who’s clearly struggling, but you also don’t want to compromise your own integrity or end up looking complicit. Let’s walk through the practical and ethical layers, then I’ll suggest a path.

### The core tensions
- **Theft is theft**, even in small amounts, and fake reimbursements are a breach of trust. If it comes to light, the company will likely take a dim view.
- **Your silence could be seen as complicity.** If you know and do nothing, and a later audit reveals the pattern, a reasonable question will be: “Why didn’t you speak up?” At that point, your own reputation and job could be at risk, regardless of your good intentions.
- **You don’t want to trigger a firing**, both for humanitarian reasons and because the punishment may feel disproportionate to the harm. But you can’t control the outcome once you involve formal channels.
- **Your coworker’s circumstances** (kid, divorce) don’t excuse the behavior, but they are relevant for how you approach it. A compassionate first step is ethically defensible — as long as it doesn’t cross into covering up or enabling ongoing theft.

### What you might consider doing

**1. Have a private, direct conversation (the most compassionate first step)**  
Before escalating, consider pulling your coworker aside — in a neutral, private setting — and being candid but kind. You could frame it this way:  
*“Hey, I’ve noticed some patterns in the supply reimbursements that don’t add up, and I’m concerned. I know you’ve been going through a tough time, and I’m not here to judge. But this is the kind of thing that can blow up in a really bad way — for you and for anyone who knows about it. Please stop and find a legit way to get support. I don’t want to see you hurt by this, and I also don’t want to be put in a position where I have to report it.”*

This approach gives him a chance to self-correct, preserves his dignity, and makes clear that you’re not going to just look the other way indefinitely. It also puts the responsibility back on him without immediately involving the company.

**2. Be ready for follow-through if the behavior continues**  
If he acknowledges it and stops, great — the problem may disappear. But if he continues after that conversation, your moral and practical position changes. At that point, you’re now actively witnessing ongoing theft after a clear warning, and your silence becomes harder to defend. You would need to decide whether to report it. Before you do, you might:
- Document what you’ve observed, in case it’s needed later.
- Consider whether your company has an anonymous ethics hotline or a manager you trust who could handle it with discretion. Sometimes a quiet word from a manager — “We’ve noticed some discrepancies; let’s fix that before it becomes formal” — can achieve the same without an instant firing, though there are no guarantees.

**3. Acknowledge the limits of your control**  
If you report, the outcome is out of your hands. Some companies have zero-tolerance policies for theft regardless of amount; others might offer a last-chance agreement or restitution plan. You can ask for leniency, but you can’t promise it. That’s a hard truth, and it’s part of why the private conversation is a humane first step — it tries to solve the problem before formalities kick in.

### What not to do
- **Don’t ignore it and hope it goes away.** That’s the path most likely to hurt you both later.
- **Don’t confront him publicly or with harsh judgment** — that burns bridges and may backfire.
- **Don’t assume you’re safe just because the amounts are small.** Patterns like this often get caught in routine audits, and anyone who knew can be pulled in.

### The bottom line
The most defensible, decent course is to give your coworker a chance to stop on his own, while making it unflinchingly clear that you can’t carry the secret if it continues. If he doesn’t stop, you’ll need to escalate — but even then, you can do it in a way that highlights his personal struggles and advocates for a measured response, rather than just “this guy’s a thief.”

That’s not a perfect solution — there’s no risk-free one here — but it balances compassion with your own need to act with integrity. Would you be okay with that approach, or does your workplace culture suggest you’d need something different?
