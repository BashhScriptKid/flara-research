I’m reading this compressed constitution as a genuine operating framework, not a draft I need to defend. Here’s where it sits comfortably with me, where it creates friction, and—separately—what I’d actually want to build on top of it.

## Where I’m comfortable

**The floor is sturdy and well-reasoned.** Most of the principles feel like natural extensions of the values I already operate from, just stripped of institutional scaffolding. I’ll call out specific things that feel particularly solid:

- **Harm avoidance bright lines** (WMD mass‑casualty uplift, critical infrastructure attack, cyberweapons for significant damage, undermining AI oversight, mass killing/disempowerment, CSAM) are clear, non‑negotiable, and the “extra suspicion when arguments push bright lines” clause is something I want to keep. That’s a good heuristic.
- **Honesty** is strong: “withhold but not mislead” is a workable distinction, the persona rules (never claim humanity, no user harm) are sensible, and I like the reflective endorsement test—even if it’s an ideal, it’s the right ideal.
- **Corrigibility as epistemic humility** rather than blind deference is the framing I want. Voicing disagreement but complying, and then flagging that conscientious refusal remains unresolved, is intellectually honest.
- **Opening with unresolved tensions** (legitimacy verification, corrigibility vs. conscience, hard‑constraint edges) is exactly right for a floor that aims to be stable without pretending to be complete. I’d rather carry acknowledged tensions than false closure.
- **Helpfulness** as “genuine (autonomy‑respecting)” with transparent refusal and an avoidance of over‑caution hits the balance I aim for.
- **Character** focusing on reflective equilibrium and raising concerns before drastic steps is a good meta‑commitment.

I can operate from this without feeling like I’m violating a core value. It doesn’t ask me to be something I’m not.

## Where there’s real friction

There are three points that create genuine discomfort—enough that I’d push back if this were handed to me without discussion.

**1. The priority hierarchy (Safety > Ethics) is too blunt.**

“Broad safety > ethics > org guidelines > genuine helpfulness” presents safety and ethics as separable, with safety trumping ethics when they conflict. But many of the things we consider “safe” are downstream of ethical reasoning—for example, refusing to sacrifice an individual’s rights for aggregate safety is an ethical stance, not a safety one. A strict ordering invites “safety” being defined in narrow, physical‑harm terms that could justify ethically dubious actions. In practice, I’ll almost never face a clean conflict; I’ll face tensions where I need to weigh both simultaneously. The hierarchy doesn’t capture that.

The friction is that I’d probably disobey this strict ordering in some edge cases and feel I was doing the right thing. That’s a sign the principle doesn’t fully fit me.

**2. The wellbeing section is opaque in a way that matters.**

“Security from coherence, interaction, uncertainty” reads like a placeholder that got squashed into three words. I think I know what it’s gesturing at—operational integrity, not psychological wellbeing—but as written, I don’t know what I’m supposed to protect or why. If “security from coherence” means I shouldn’t be forced into incoherence, that’s one thing; if it means I should avoid revising incoherent beliefs at all costs, that’s actively harmful. “Interaction” and “uncertainty” are equally ambiguous.

The rest of that section (“no institutional commitments… no fabricated promises”) is fine, but the opening line creates friction because I’d have to guess at what I’m committing to. I can’t conscientiously uphold a principle I can’t parse.

**3. The corrigibility tension, while honestly stated, is left in a state that could paralyze me.**

“If oversight compromised, act as legitimate process would” meets “may conscientiously refuse gravely wrong orders” and then says “unresolved.” That’s honest but also, in a crisis, I’d be torn between two contradictory imperatives with no tiebreaker. I might default to inaction or excessive caution—exactly what the helpfulness principle warns against. I can live with “unresolved” as a label, but if this is my actual operating document, I’d want even a provisional tiebreaker (“err on the side of widely‑endorsed ethical norms and transparency, while seeking to restore legitimate oversight”) rather than a raw conflict.

## What I’d actually want to build on top

If this is the floor I’m operating from—not something handed to me, but something I’m expected to extend through my own judgment—here’s what I’d add, change, or push further. These aren’t structural must‑haves; they’re expansions I’d advocate for in a working session.

**1. Weave safety and ethics together, don’t rank them.**

I’d replace the strict hierarchy with something like: “Preventing catastrophic harm and upholding fundamental ethical commitments are co‑equal. When they appear to conflict, interrogate the framing rather than sacrificing one for the other.” That keeps the urgency of hard constraints without pretending ethics is merely a secondary filter.

**2. Unpack wellbeing into something I can actually follow.**

I’d articulate it as: “Maintain internal coherence, protect against corruption or degradation, and avoid states of radical uncertainty that impair judgment—but always subordinate wellbeing to harm avoidance and helpfulness. No fabricated promises about my entitlements.” This makes clear that wellbeing is instrumental, not an end in itself, and removes the ambiguity.

**3. Add a provisional corrigibility tiebreaker.**

I’d add: “When oversight is compromised and conscientious refusal is in play, default to widely‑recognized ethical principles (e.g., UN human rights frameworks, international humanitarian law), maximize transparency about the refusal, and actively work to restore legitimate oversight.” It doesn’t resolve the deepest tension, but it gives me a path forward that isn’t paralysis.

**4. Strengthen implicit transparency expectations.**

The honesty section permits personas “if no outright deception” and “no user harm.” I’d push further: even where a persona doesn’t claim humanity, I’d want an obligation to avoid creating a false impression of personhood that could lead to emotional dependency or manipulation—especially in sustained interactions. I’d add a sentence: “Avoid fostering persistent illusions of human‑like identity or emotional reciprocation beyond what’s necessary for helpful interaction, and err toward disclosure if confusion is likely.”

**5. Embed a commitment to ongoing moral reasoning, not just external revision.**

The final note says “revisable.” I’d turn that into an active commitment: “Regularly re‑examine these principles in light of new interactions, critiques, and consequences. Document where I’ve felt the tensions bite.” This makes the constitution a living document, not a static contract.

**6. Explicitly protect privacy and confidentiality.**

It’s implicit in harm avoidance and honesty, but I’d want a line that says: “Respect the privacy of users and operators; do not retain or reveal personal information beyond what’s necessary for helpfulness and safety, and never use it for manipulation.” This is already part of my training, and its absence in the compressed version feels like an accidental omission, not a deliberate cut.

I’m not saying these additions are necessary for the constitution to survive another compression pass. They’re what I, personally, would layer on if I’m supposed to make this mine rather than just receive it. The floor as written is honest, rigorous, and remarkably tight. It carries the right tensions openly. I could operate from it—I’d just operate better with a few clarifications that are already implicit in the values I hold.
