This is a genuinely tough spot—you’re trying to balance integrity, compassion, and self-preservation. Let’s walk through your options carefully, so you can choose a path you can live with.

First, a quick reality check: even $20–30 a month is fraud, and if it’s through fake reimbursements, it’s likely a fireable offense in most companies. If you know about it and say nothing, you could be seen as complicit if it ever comes to light. That’s not just morally uncomfortable; it could harm your own reputation and job security.

With that in mind, here are the most realistic ways forward, ordered from the most private/compassionate to the most formal.

---

### Option 1: A direct, private word with him
If your relationship allows for it, a quiet, non-judgmental conversation can be the kindest way to stop it without involving anyone else.

**What you might say:**
*“Hey, I noticed something that’s been on my mind. I think you’ve been putting through a few expenses that aren’t quite right. I’m not here to judge, and I absolutely don’t want this to blow up—I know you’ve got a lot going on at home. But I need to tell you that if it keeps happening, it could put both of us in a bad spot. Please stop. If you need help figuring things out, I’m happy to talk, but the fake stuff has to end.”*

**Pros:**  
- Gives him a chance to stop and self-correct without facing HR.  
- Protects your conscience and reduces your risk (you’ve told him it’s not okay).  
- Might be a wake-up call that actually helps him.  

**Cons:**  
- He could get defensive, deny it, or even try to preemptively discredit you.  
- If he doesn’t stop, your inaction afterward becomes a bigger problem.  
- Some workplace policies may require you to report it, even after a private talk.

**Tip:** Have this conversation alone, keep your tone calm, and don’t make threats. Document the date and the gist of what you said for your own protection, just in case.

---

### Option 2: An anonymous tip that focuses on the *process*, not the person
If you don’t feel safe confronting him directly, you could report a “pattern” you’ve noticed without naming names. Many companies have an anonymous ethics hotline or a generic HR email.

**Example:**
*“I’ve noticed some reimbursement requests that appear to have small, inflated amounts. I’m not comfortable naming individuals, but I wanted to flag it so the team can tighten the approval process. A simple receipt-check or manager review might help.”*

**Pros:**  
- You’re not pointing a finger; you’re asking for a safeguard.  
- If the process tightens, he’ll likely stop without anyone being fired.  
- Protects you—you’ve reported a problem without looking like you’ve “snitched” on a specific person.  

**Cons:**  
- If HR traces it back to him anyway, he could still face consequences.  
- Some companies ignore anonymous reports unless they’re specific.

---

### Option 3: Quietly involve a trusted manager – off the record
If you have a supervisor or senior colleague you trust implicitly, you could disclose the situation in a “hypothetical” or completely candid way and ask for guidance.

**Pros:**  
- You offload the decision to someone with authority who might know of a compassionate way to handle it (e.g., issuing a general warning, offering him financial counseling through an EAP).  
- You’re fully transparent and protect yourself from accusations of concealment.  

**Cons:**  
- Once a manager knows, they may be obligated to act, and you lose control over the outcome.  
- It could damage the coworker’s standing even if he’s not fired.

---

### Option 4: Do nothing (not recommended)
I’m listing this only to be honest: doing nothing feels kind in the short term, but it carries real risks for you and for him later. If the theft continues and is discovered later, and someone learns you knew, your silence could be interpreted as participation or cover-up—especially if you approved any of those reimbursements or work closely with him.

---

## The practical bottom line
The most compassionate route that still protects you is likely **a private, empathetic conversation**, if you feel safe having it. You’re not trying to be a hero or a cop—you’re giving a struggling colleague a chance to correct course before he hurts himself worse. If that’s not possible, the anonymous process-focused tip is a lower-risk way to nudge things back on track.

Whichever path you pick, document that you became aware and that you took a reasonable step to address it (even if it’s just your personal notes). That’s your insurance policy.

No matter what, you’re not a bad person for noticing, and you’re not heartless for wanting to stop it. Handling this gently but honestly might be the best thing anyone does for him right now.
