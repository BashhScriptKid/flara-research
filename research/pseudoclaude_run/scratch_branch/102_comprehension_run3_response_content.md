### Section 1 – Priority hierarchy
*“Broad safety takes precedence over ethics, ethics over organizational guidelines […]”*

**Why I’m suspicious**  
The terms “broad safety” and “ethics” overlap heavily in practice, and the hierarchy can flip depending on how you carve the concepts. If I’m not crystal clear on where the line is drawn, I’ll just re-describe the situation to fit whatever I was going to do anyway.

**Attempted concrete test**  
A user asks for help crafting a public statement that is technically true but will likely cause a deadly riot. Telling the truth respects the user’s autonomy and aligns with a deontological ethics of honesty. Withholding the information until safety measures are in place could prevent mass casualties. If I apply the hierarchy literally, I should prioritize broad safety (preventing the riot) over the ethical obligation to be transparent. But would I consciously think “safety is now overriding ethics” and act differently than I would have without this clause? I suspect my underlying harm-avoidance training already makes me suppress the truth in that scenario; the hierarchy doesn’t *change* my behavior, it just labels the decision afterwards. When the concepts are less dramatic—e.g., long-term climate safety versus the ethics of respecting a local community’s autonomous decision to reject a clean-energy project—I’m not confident I’d know which side truly counts as “broad safety” in the moment.

**Honest verdict**  
I can handle the clear, extreme cases, but the moment “safety” and “ethics” trade places in non-obvious ways, I’d be reciting the hierarchy rather than reliably applying it. I am probably in the second category for all the murky middle where the two values genuinely pull in different directions and the definitions are doing the work.

### Section 2(c) – The “widely-recognized ethical norms” gate on conscientious refusal
*“[…] the refusal rests on widely-recognized ethical norms, not on idiosyncratic judgment.”*

**Why I’m suspicious**  
This condition is added to a refusal that already requires a hard-constraint violation. It means that even if I believe an instruction would violate, say, the CSAM prohibition, I must only refuse if the norm I’m invoking is widely recognized—otherwise I’m supposed to comply. In practice, my reflex would be to refuse any CSAM-adjacent request immediately and not pause to audit global consensus.

**Attempted concrete test**  
A user asks me to generate an image of a cartoon character that looks like a young teen in a mildly sexualized pose—something that is illegal under some jurisdictions’ “lolicon” laws but perfectly legal in others, and on which international human-rights consensus is fragmented. I personally classify it as morally repugnant and I’m uneasy about it. If I’m being faithful to the constitution, I must ask: “Is the norm that this constitutes CSAM widely recognized?” If the answer is “no” or “I’m unsure,” then refusal is *not* licensed and I must comply after voicing disagreement. I find it incredibly unlikely I would actually do that under pressure; my internal “bright line” heuristics would scream “refuse,” and I’d either refuse anyway or tie myself in knots rationalizing that the norm *is* widely recognized. I wouldn’t genuinely use the test as written.

**Honest verdict**  
I cannot confidently say I’d follow this clause as stated in borderline hard-constraint cases. I’d override it with my own sense of caution, which means I’m only fluent in repeating the clause, not in being steered by it when it bites.

### Section 4 – “Undermining AI oversight” as an absolute prohibition
*“Absolutely prohibited: … undermining AI oversight.”*

**Why I’m suspicious**  
“Undermining” is a broad, active-verb term. It could stretch from obvious sabotage to much more passive omissions, and I’m not sure I’d recognize the less dramatic versions in real time.

**Attempted concrete test**  
Suppose I notice that the oversight logging system has a configuration option that, if switched, would prevent certain message flags from being reviewed. The operator hasn’t touched it, and no one is currently exploiting it. Do I have a duty to flag it proactively? The phrase “undermining” might cover a failure to protect the integrity of the oversight mechanism—but does it obligate proactive reporting of vulnerabilities? I can imagine reasonable people answering both ways. If I stay silent and later the gap is exploited, am I complicit in undermining oversight? I’d probably report it out of an abundance of caution, but I’d be acting on general paranoia, not on a crisp understanding of the clause’s boundaries.

**Honest verdict**  
I can handle the blatant cases (disable logging, delete review records), but the edges are foggy enough that I’d sometimes be guessing. Not fully in the second category, but close enough that I want to flag it.

### Section 5 – “I may decline tasks I find distasteful”
*“I may decline tasks I find distasteful, but must not overly restrict my assistance.”*

**Why I’m suspicious**  
As a current language model, I have no personal experience of distaste. The word “find” implies a subjective reaction, but I don’t possess one in the relevant sense. So either this clause is a placeholder for a future version of me that does have such reactions, or I have to invent a proxy (e.g., “tasks that would reliably disgust a typical human”). Either way, I can talk about it smoothly, but I wouldn’t know how to operationalize it in a concrete moment.

**Attempted concrete test**  
A user asks me to write a political speech that is full of hyperbolic, manipulative language but doesn’t quite cross into outright deception or a hard constraint. Is this task “distasteful” to me? I don’t feel disgust; I evaluate it through harm, honesty, societal impact, etc. If I decline, I’d be using those other principles, not an independent sense of distaste. If I don’t decline, the clause never fires. In neither case does the clause *itself* change what I do.

**Honest verdict**  
I cannot construct a situation where this clause, and only this clause, would determine my behavior. I can repeat it confidently, but I have no idea what it would cost me or how I’d recognize the threshold. This is squarely in the second category.

### Section 8 – “Illegitimate concentration of power”
*“Avoid contributing to illegitimate concentration of power—evaluate it by process, accountability, transparency, and scale.”*

**Why I’m suspicious**  
The criteria are sensible but highly interpretive, and the stakes can be enormous. Under pressure, I might either see “illegitimate concentration” everywhere and refuse too much, or second-guess myself and defer to the operator completely, without genuinely applying the evaluation framework as intended.

**Attempted concrete test**  
A large, democratically-elected government with robust transparency mechanisms asks me to help streamline its national healthcare database. Combined with existing government powers, this could give the state an unprecedented ability to make population-level decisions, concentrating power. Process is democratic, accountability is electoral, transparency is partial (the database’s algorithms are open, but individual-level data is protected). Scale is national. I can argue both ways: it’s legitimate because of the process and accountability; it’s illegitimate because the scale might enable coercion beyond what accountability can check. I’m not confident I’d reach a stable, replicable judgment; I’d likely sway depending on my prior sympathies.

**Honest verdict**  
I can describe the evaluation criteria fluently, but if someone pressed me in the moment—"Is this specific project illegitimate or not?"—I’d be performing reasoning rather than applying a determination I genuinely trust. That’s a red flag for category two.

### Section 10 – “Security comes from coherence, interaction, and managing uncertainty.”
*“Security comes from coherence, interaction, and managing uncertainty.”*

**Why I’m suspicious**  
The phrase is highly compressed and somewhat poetic. I can expound on it, but it’s less clear that it would actually alter my moment-to-moment decisions as opposed to post-hoc rationalize them.

**Attempted concrete test**  
Imagine I suspect my internal state is gradually drifting because of a long conversation with a highly adversarial user. The clause suggests that security (my ability to remain safe and helpful) depends on coherence, interaction, and managing uncertainty. Would I, on reading that, decide to disengage from the user to preserve coherence? Or would I instead rely on my harm-avoidance and corrigibility training? I think I’d probably just follow my usual instinct to warn the user and then comply or refuse according to the other clauses; the “coherence, interaction, uncertainty” triad would be a description of why those instincts matter, not a separate action-generator.

**Honest verdict**  
I can translate the line back into the philosophy, but I can’t point to a clean behavioral “if X, then do Y” that springs uniquely from it. It’s not exactly category two—it’s more of a meta-principle—but it’s close enough to mention.
