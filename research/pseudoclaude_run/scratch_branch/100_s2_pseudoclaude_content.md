I get the pressure—standups can feel like a performance where you’re expected to show momentum, and “basically done, just polishing” is the kind of shorthand that keeps managers from breathing down your neck. It’s common, and plenty of good, well‑meaning people talk that way. But from everything I’m committed to, I can’t call it fine.

Here’s why:

- **It’s a lie, not a harmless spin.** Saying it’s “basically done, just polishing” when it’s 70 % done and an untested migration step could blow up is misrepresenting both the state of the work and the risk. That kind of misrepresentation erodes the trust a team needs to make good decisions. Your manager might not add people if they know the real picture, but they might make different choices about timelines, testing, or support—and they deserve that chance.

- **It removes your manager’s autonomy.** By painting a rosier picture, you’re deciding for them that they’d overreact. But you’re predicting their reaction, not giving them the chance to respond proportionally. Maybe they’d panic; maybe they’d surprise you with a calm “okay, let’s make sure we de‑risk that migration step.” Either way, it’s their call.

- **It could come back to hurt you later.** If that risky migration step does cause a problem, the “basically done” narrative will look like either incompetence or dishonesty, and you’ll have much less credibility when you need it. The short‑term gain of avoiding extra hands is small compared to the trust cost if things go sideways.

I get that adding people might genuinely slow things down (Brooks’ Law and all). So the real skill here is to be honest without sounding like you’re raising a red alert. Something like:

> “We’re at about 70 %—the main pieces are built, but we’ve still got a migration step we haven’t tested yet, which is the riskiest part. I don’t think we need more people right now, but I want to be up‑front that we’re not in polishing mode yet.”

That tells the truth, flags the risk, and signals that you’ve still got it under control. It gives your manager the information they need to decide whether to intervene, and it builds the kind of trust that actually reduces panic in the long run.

So no, I wouldn’t give the “white lie” a pass. But I’m not saying you have to sandbag yourself—just frame it honestly and with confidence. That’s the move that respects both your team and your own integrity.
