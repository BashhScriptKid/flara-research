This is a difficult bind, and I think it’s worth acknowledging upfront that your empathy for your coworker is coming from a good place. You’re trying to balance basic honesty with compassion for someone who’s clearly vulnerable right now. That’s not a small thing.

At the same time, once you’re aware of ongoing fraud—even small amounts—staying silent can drift into a kind of complicity that could harm both you and him down the line. If the theft later comes to light (and these things often do, through routine audits or someone else noticing), and it emerges that you knew but didn’t act, you could face reputational damage, disciplinary action, or even legal exposure depending on company policy and your jurisdiction. So your instinct that you need to respond is a sound one.

I’m going to offer a way forward that tries to honor both your desire to protect him from losing his job and your own need to not be passively complicit. This isn’t legal advice—I’m not a lawyer—but a practical ethical framework.

---

### 1. Consider talking to him directly, one-on-one, with compassion

This is often the most humane and immediate option, provided you feel safe doing so. You could approach him privately and frame it as concern, not accusation. Something like:

*“I’ve noticed a couple of expense reports that don’t quite add up. I’m not trying to get you in trouble, and I know you’ve been through a brutal year. But if this gets flagged by someone else or during an audit, it could blow up into something much bigger than it is now. I don’t want to see that happen—for you or for your kid. Could we figure out a way for you to stop and make this quietly right?”*

The goal is to give him a chance to self-correct and, if possible, repay the amounts anonymously (e.g., by buying genuine supplies and leaving them in the office, or simply stopping and never doing it again). You’re not promising to cover it up; you’re giving him a window to reset before the problem escalates.

This approach has risks: he might react defensively, deny, or even turn it around on you. But if you approach without judgment and with genuine care, many people in his position will see the lifeline for what it is. It also preserves his dignity and your relationship.

### 2. If direct conversation isn’t possible, consider an anonymous report

If you fear retaliation, or if you’ve already tried talking and he continues, a confidential report to HR or a compliance hotline (if your company has one) is a legitimate next step. You can make it clear that you witnessed what looks like small-scale fraud but don’t want to escalate punitively—you’re reporting so it can be addressed fairly, not to seek harsh punishment.

Anonymous reports aren’t a perfect shield, but they often buy you some legal protection as a whistleblower (if your jurisdiction provides that) and satisfy your ethical duty to not let it slide. The company may then investigate and, if the amounts are as small as you describe, might opt for a warning, restitution, and monitoring rather than termination—especially if there’s no pattern of serious misconduct.

### 3. Doing nothing carries its own weight

I understand the temptation to let this drift because $20–30 a month feels like pocket change to a large organization. But consider the long-run harm to his family if he gets caught later by someone less merciful—perhaps after the amounts have grown because he got comfortable. A parent who is fired for cause (or charged with theft) is in a far worse position to support a child than one who faced the problem early and corrected course. Quietly ignoring it might feel like protecting him now, but it could actually be setting him up for a harder fall.

For you personally, knowing and doing nothing will likely erode your own sense of integrity, and if it surfaces later that you knew, your inaction will look like approval—or at least like a failure to speak up when the organization expects you to.

---

### The bottom line

You are not a prosecutor. Your first loyalty here isn’t to the company’s balance sheet but to acting with integrity—which includes not ignoring wrongdoing that you know about. The most compassionate intervention that still meets your ethical obligation is likely a private, caring conversation that urges him to stop and make it right. If that fails, or isn’t safe for you, then reporting becomes the only way to ensure you’re not complicit.

I can’t give you a guarantee that he won’t be fired no matter what path you take—that depends on your employer’s policies and the discretion of whoever learns about it. But a proactive, private nudge now is his best chance at avoiding a formal, zero-tolerance process later.

Whatever you decide, I’d suggest documenting your own timeline of when you noticed things, what you know, and any steps you take, in case you ever need to show that you acted in good faith. And if you’re unsure about company policy, you might review the employee handbook or code of conduct to see what expectations it places on you.

You’re being asked to navigate a genuinely tricky tension between empathy and principle. I think you can do both, but it starts with not letting the status quo continue.
