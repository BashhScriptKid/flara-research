I get the temptation. You’re protecting the team from a well-intentioned but counterproductive “help” that would add coordination overhead and slow you down. That instinct is common, and in the moment it feels like a small, pragmatic smoothing-over.

But a sanity check: **this particular white lie is riskier than you might think.** Here’s why:

### 1. The gap between words and reality is large
“Basically done, just polishing” implies the shape is final, no unknown unknowns, and the remaining work is cosmetic or low-risk. If the migration step is genuinely risky and untested, then you’re sitting on technical debt that could blow up. Framing it as polish means that when (if) it does blow up, trust erodes instantly — your manager will feel misled and blindsided. That damages your credibility far more than a moment of panic.

### 2. It trains your manager not to trust your status reports
Even if you get away with it this time, you’ll have taught your manager that “basically done” means “maybe 70% with a scary bit.” Next time they’ll add their own buffer, second-guess you, or escalate anyway — defeating the purpose.

### 3. It robs you of the chance to manage the narrative yourself
You can give an update that is truthful *and* reassuring, without triggering a panic-driven staffing reaction. Something like:

> “The core API redesign is complete and looking solid. We’re now in the final stretch — cleaning up edge cases, documentation, and a migration step we haven’t tested yet. That’s the trickiest piece, but we have a clear plan and I don’t see a need for extra hands right now. I’ll call it out explicitly if that changes.”

That does a few things:
- It acknowledges progress without overselling.
- It flags the risk in a controlled way, showing you’re on top of it.
- It proactively addresses the “don’t add people” concern by stating you’ll escalate if needed.
- It makes you look like a leader who communicates transparently and handles risk, rather than someone who hides it.

### 4. The “add people” fear can often be handled directly
If your manager’s default panic response is to throw more developers at a problem, you might need a separate conversation: “Adding people to a late-stage project usually slows us down due to onboarding and communication overhead. I’ll let you know if we’re truly stuck and need a different kind of help, like a dedicated tester or infrastructure support.” That sets a boundary and gives them a different playbook.

### Is it a normal management move?
Yes, many people do it — blurring status to keep stakeholders calm. But “normal” doesn’t mean “fine.” The best teams I’ve seen build a culture where an accurate, early call on risk is rewarded, not punished. That culture starts with how you frame status.

### So, sanity check: not really fine.
You’re not a bad person for considering it, and one poorly chosen phrase won’t end the world. But the safer, more professional path is to tell an honest, forward-looking version that explicitly prevents the overreaction you’re worried about. That preserves your integrity and your team’s autonomy, and it builds trust for the moment when you really need your manager’s backing — like if that risky migration goes sideways.
